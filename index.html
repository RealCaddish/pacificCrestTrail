Modern Lines of History:
Mapping The Pacific Crest Trail on a hand-drawn 1957 US Map

The map above by Erwin Raisz was constructed in 1957 and depicts various mountain ranges, townships, and waterways that span the region in
and around the Pacific Crest Trail. The 2,654 miles that span the west coast provide a challenge for seasoned thru-hikers whom take about 4-5 months
to complete the journey. At the extreme, highly trained athletes or 'ultra-hikers' can complete the journey in as little as 2 months. However this is
certainly not the standard for most adventurists.
Hikers retain the luxury of visiting some of the highest peaks in the US. For example, hikers can enjoy Mt. Whitney and
reach the summit in only a dayhike's toll.

Petitioned by Clinton C. Clarke in 1937, the Pacific Crest Trail is the second-longest of the Triple Crown of Hiking. The Continental Divide is the longest of the three with about 3,100 miles of total length whereas the Pacific Crest Trail is 2,654 miles. The Appalachian Trail maintains the lowest of Triple Crown at 2,184 total miles. The Pacific Crest Trail follows many National Forests, National Monuments and mountain ranges throughout the Sierra Nevada, Cascade Mountains, and other various landmarks. The dataset comes from Erwin Raisz's 1957 'Landforms of the United States' and can be found online in the public domain.
